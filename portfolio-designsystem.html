GOV.UK One Login Design System Foundations

Laying the groundwork for consistency, alignment and scale across a complex, fast-paced government service.

Quick Overview

  • Introduced a design system approach for GOV.UK One Login, spanning web, mobile and in-person journeys
  • Built an interactive prototype — now critical for onboarding, training and service readiness
  • Strengthened Figma, mobile design patterns and frontend alignment
  • Surfaced hidden design and product debt, making gaps visible and manageable
  • Helped teams align and deliver consistently — without slowing delivery

The Challenge

GOV.UK One Login helps people prove their identity and access services across web, mobile and in-person journeys.

As the programme scaled quickly, predictable system gaps appeared:

  • Design patterns drifting across pods
  • Mobile design evolving separately from shared components
  • Inconsistent handover to development
  • Limited visibility of end-to-end journeys
  • Design and product debt building quietly

We had GOV.UK Design System foundations — but One Login needed clearer structure to scale effectively.

My Role

  • Mapped real system gaps — not just surface design issues
  • Used roadmap leverage points to introduce practical improvements
  • Applied technical understanding to build trust across disciplines
  • Strengthened design tools, processes and handover without slowing delivery
  • Surfaced hidden debt as visible, manageable product gaps

How I Navigated It

1. Making Complexity Visible — with an MVP Prototype

The issue: Teams lacked a shared, reliable view of how journeys connected — especially as mobile, web and in-person work scaled.

What I did:

  • Identified roadmap opportunities to introduce an interactive prototype
  • Built realistic, clickable journeys across web and mobile — focusing on happy paths first
  • Applied technical detail so the prototype reflected how the service actually behaved
  • Positioned the prototype as a practical tool for teams — not just a design deliverable

The result: Quickly adopted as a critical tool for onboarding, training, spotting gaps and service readiness checks.

2. Strengthening Design Tools — Building Structure into Daily Work

The issue: Figma use was inconsistent. Mobile design patterns weren’t connected to the wider system. Handover to development varied.

What I did:

  • Ran discovery to map design system pain points across pods
  • Introduced clear structure to Figma: file standards, naming, handover improvements
  • Built a mobile design system aligned with frontend components
  • Established a contribution model for shared libraries
  • Surfaced remaining gaps as product debt, managed in the wider roadmap

The result: More reliable design foundations — reducing drift, improving handover, supporting scale.

3. Aligning Design and Development — Through Structured Collaboration

The issue: Design-to-development alignment was inconsistent, with gaps often hidden as delivery scaled.

What I did:

  • Partnered with tech and QA leads to embed alignment into delivery
  • Introduced visual snapshot tests to catch inconsistencies early
  • Revisited shared component use across teams to reduce drift
  • Used roadmap milestones to coordinate improvements

The result: Fewer inconsistencies, improved shared understanding, alignment built into delivery — not bolted on.

Reflections

Scaling a complex service isn’t about adding process. It’s about understanding system gaps, using the right moments to introduce structure, and building alignment through delivery.

Making complexity visible, strengthening tools and surfacing product debt has helped One Login scale with more confidence — supporting teams and ensuring the service stays accessible and consistent for users.
